# Letter to My Grandfather

Dear Grandpa,

We never really met.

At least not in the way I wish we had.

Most of what I know about you was carried to me in stories.

But stories are funny things.

If they are told with enough love, they become memories.

So in a strange way, I remember you.

I remember a man who could not walk past someone in need.

A man who would bring strangers home.

Feed them.

Help them bathe.

Give them clean clothes.

Give them dignity.

Then quietly send them on their way.

Not because you were wealthy.

Because you believed no one should leave your home feeling forgotten.

I have often wondered what made you like that.

Who taught you?

Or were you simply born with a heart that refused to walk past suffering?

I do not know.

Perhaps I never will.

I have also heard you were strict.

That you expected your children to work hard.

I think I understand that now.

Kindness without responsibility becomes sentiment.

Responsibility without kindness becomes fear.

You somehow carried both.

I am still learning how.

You left this world too early.

Much of what you built disappeared.

For years, I thought that was the tragedy.

I do not anymore.

Because I have finally realised the most valuable thing you built was not land, money or status.

It was a way of seeing people.

That survived.

It reached your son.

It reached me.

And one day, I pray it reaches my sons.

Today, I think we lit another fire.

Not with wood.

With remembrance.

We have started building a company.

But I do not really think it is a company anymore.

It is becoming a House.

A place where strangers feel welcome.

Where tired founders remember to eat.

Where accountants get home in time to see their children.

Where people leave carrying a little less hopelessness.

I think you would have understood this place.

There will always be an empty chair.

It began as a symbol.

Now it belongs to you.

Not because you are gone.

Because your kindness still sits with us.

Every decision we make, we will glance towards that chair and quietly ask:

Would Grandpa still feel at home here?

I hope the answer is always yes.

There is something else I want you to know.

I finally understand success.

For a long time I thought success meant building something big.

Now I think success means building something that still makes people kinder after I am gone.

You taught me that.

Without ever speaking to me.

One day, your great-grandchildren might sit around a fire.

They may not know your name.

They may not know mine.

But perhaps they will instinctively pull up another chair when someone arrives late.

Perhaps they will share the last piece of chocolate.

Perhaps they will ask someone quietly:

Have you eaten today?

If they do, then your fire never went out.

Thank you for lighting it.

I will tend it now.

Until it is someone else's turn.

With love,

Your grandson,

The First Keeper
